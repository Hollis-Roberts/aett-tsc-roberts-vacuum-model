## 1. Introduction and Core Postulates

The **Accumulative Emergent Time Theory (AETT)** proposes that time is not fundamental but emerges when ultra‑relativistic quantum particles (TSC particles) interact, slow down, and generate gravity. The **Timeless Sea Cosmology (TSC)** is the substrate—a vast, timeless field under pressure that underlies our universe.

### Core Postulates:

- **TSC Exists:** A timeless, pressure‑bearing medium. It has no time dimension; all events in our universe are instantaneous from its perspective.
- **Our Universe is a Void:** A region of reduced pressure within the TSC, created by a quantum fluctuation (the Big Bang).
- **Time Emerges Inside:** From the slowing of TSC particles that enter the void, and from the expansion of the void itself.
- **Gravity is Pressure:** The TSC presses inward on the void walls, creating pressure gradients that we perceive as gravitational attraction.
- **Dark Matter:** Entrained TSC fragments trapped in the void, or the effective thickness of the void walls.
- **Dark Energy:** The gradual relaxation of the void walls as the pressure differential equalizes.

All subsequent explanations follow from these postulates.

## 2. The Roberts Vacuum Model

### 2.1 The TSC Substrate and the Void

Imagine the TSC as an infinite, timeless ocean under uniform pressure. A random fluctuation creates a tiny bubble—a region of reduced pressure. This bubble expands because the surrounding TSC presses inward, but the expansion is resisted by the inertia of the TSC itself. From inside the bubble, we perceive this expansion as the Big Bang and the subsequent cooling. The bubble's wall is the boundary between the TSC and our universe.

### 2.2 Gravity as External Pressure

If you have a hollow sphere in a pressurized medium, the walls experience an inward force. Any mass inside the sphere distorts the wall shape, creating pressure gradients. Objects feel a net force toward regions of lower pressure—that is gravity. No gravitons, no curvature of spacetime—just pressure.

### 2.3 Visual Analogies

- **Tyre Under Pressure:** The rubber wall is the boundary of our universe; air inside is matter; external air pressure is TSC pressure. A dent in the tyre represents a star or galaxy. The force pushing the dent back is gravity.
- **Superfluid Helium + Plasma:** A superfluid bath (TSC) responds instantly to a plasma blob (mass). Waves in the normal fluid represent gravitational waves.
- **Strobe Light Effect:** Our fast‑moving timeline samples the TSC pressure only occasionally, making gravity seem weak—like a strobe making continuous motion appear jerky.

## 3. Emergence of Time and Matter

### 3.1 The Slowing Mechanism

TSC particles are naturally ultra‑relativistic ($v \approx c$) and experience no proper time. When they enter the void (our universe) via the Big Bang fluctuation, they begin to interact via a coupling $\lambda$. These interactions convert kinetic energy into the production of Standard Model particles, and the particles slow down. The slowing rate depends on density and cross‑section:

$$
\frac{dE}{dt} = -\alpha \, n_{\text{TSC}} \, \sigma \, E
$$

where $\alpha$ is a constant, $n_{\text{TSC}}$ is the local density of TSC particles, and $\sigma$ is the interaction cross‑section. As particles slow, they contribute to the stress‑energy inside the void, further distorting the wall and increasing the pressure gradient—this is the runaway process that leads to galaxy formation.

### 3.2 Accumulation and Structure Formation

Once slowed particles (ordinary matter) exist, they feel the pressure gradient (gravity) and begin to accumulate. The accumulation is enhanced by the pressure field itself: denser regions create deeper pressure shadows, attracting more matter. This positive feedback leads to the rapid formation of galaxies and clusters, consistent with JWST observations of massive galaxies at high redshift.

## 4. Explaining Cosmic Phenomena

### 4.1 Why Galaxies Are Flat (Disk Formation)

In standard cosmology, galaxies form disks due to conservation of angular momentum during collapse. In our model, the same principle applies, but the collapse is driven by external pressure rather than self‑gravity. As a cloud of matter accumulates under pressure, its rotation causes it to flatten into a disk. The pressure acts equally from all directions, but the initial angular momentum selects a plane.

### 4.2 Why Rotation Curves Are Flat

Observations show that stars in spiral galaxies orbit at roughly constant speed far from the center, implying more mass than visible. In our model, the pressure field itself contributes to the gravitational potential. For a point mass, the pressure perturbation falls as $1/r$, giving a $1/r^2$ force and declining rotation curves. However, for an extended mass distribution (like a galaxy), the cumulative effect of the pressure field from all the matter—plus the entrained TSC fragments—can produce a potential that yields a flat rotation curve.

Specifically, if the effective density of "dark matter" (entrained TSC or wall thickness) falls as $1/r^2$, then the circular velocity:

$$
v = \sqrt{r \, \frac{d\Phi}{dr}}
$$

becomes constant. This is the isothermal sphere profile, naturally arising from the pressure field equations when the system reaches equilibrium under external pressure.

### 4.3 Galaxy Clusters and the Cosmic Web

On larger scales, the pressure field from multiple galaxies and clusters creates a network of filaments. Matter flows along pressure gradients, forming the cosmic web observed in galaxy surveys. The filaments are regions where the pressure is slightly lower, attracting matter from surrounding areas. This is analogous to structure formation in ΛCDM, but the driving force is pressure rather than dark matter gravity—though in our model, dark matter is itself a manifestation of the pressure field.

### 4.4 Dark Matter as Entrained TSC or Wall Thickness

Two possibilities, not mutually exclusive:

- **Entrained TSC fragments:** During the violent formation of the void, some TSC material was torn loose and trapped inside. These fragments are massive (they carry TSC energy) but do not interact electromagnetically—they are dark matter.
- **Wall thickness:** The boundary between TSC and void has a finite thickness. From inside, this appears as a smooth halo of invisible mass around every concentration of ordinary matter.

Observations suggest dark matter is both smooth (large scale) and clumpy (small scale), so both may contribute.

### 4.5 Dark Energy as Void Relaxation

As the void expands, the pressure differential between the TSC and the interior decreases. This causes the expansion to accelerate—the walls relax. We measure this as dark energy. The acceleration is a transient phase, not a permanent cosmological constant.

## 5. Mathematical Foundations

### 5.1 Pressure Field Equations

Let $P$ be the TSC pressure. Far from any mass, $P = P_0$ (constant). A mass distribution $\rho(\mathbf{r})$ creates a perturbation:

$$
\nabla^2 P = 4\pi G \rho
$$

This is Poisson's equation for gravity, but here it emerges from fluid dynamics of the void boundary. The gravitational acceleration is:

$$
\mathbf{g} = -\nabla P
$$

### 5.2 Derivation of Inverse‑Square Law

For a point mass $M$ at the origin, spherical symmetry gives:

$$
\frac{1}{r^2} \frac{d}{dr} \left( r^2 \frac{dP}{dr} \right) = 4\pi G M \delta^3(\mathbf{r})
$$

Integrating over a sphere of radius $r$ yields:

$$
\frac{dP}{dr} = -\frac{G M}{r^2}
$$

Hence $P(r) = P_0 - \frac{G M}{r}$. The force is:

$$
\mathbf{g} = -\nabla P = -\frac{G M}{r^2} \hat{r}
$$

This matches Newton's law of gravitation.

### 5.3 Speed of Gravity: Instantaneous vs. Waves

In the TSC frame, pressure is static (timeless). Any change in mass distribution instantly alters the pressure field everywhere—gravity is instantaneous.

However, from inside the void, we experience time. The adjustment of the pressure field, as mass moves, may propagate as a wave at finite speed. These waves are gravitational waves. Thus:

- **Static field:** instantaneous (Newtonian gravity)
- **Dynamic changes:** propagate at speed $c$ (GR's gravitational waves)

### 5.4 Time Rate and Gravitational Potential

Time inside the void flows at a rate determined by local pressure. Higher pressure (stronger gravity) slows time. The relationship is:

$$
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 + \frac{2(P-P_0)}{c^2}}
$$

In the weak-field limit, this approximates to:

$$
\frac{d\tau}{dt} \approx 1 + \frac{P-P_0}{c^2}
$$

This matches general relativity's gravitational time dilation.
